Energy for a Better World

Imagine a world where roads don't need constant maintenance and fuel your car with clean solar power.

The Big Idea

Modern roads are outdated and constantly breaking. What if we built roads from ultra-durable solar materials that don't just last longer‚Äîbut also power vehicles directly through contact with their tires?

Using embedded weight sensors, energy would only be transferred when a vehicle is present. It‚Äôs time we reimagine transport‚Äîcleaner, faster, and friendlier.

Materials Matter: To make this idea a reality, the road surface needs to be nearly indestructible and transparent enough to allow sunlight through. Materials like tempered laminated glass or transparent concrete could be used as top layers, providing both strength and solar transmittance.

The Physics Behind It

Photovoltaic Effect: Solar panels absorb sunlight and convert it to electricity via the photovoltaic effect using semiconductor materials (e.g., silicon).

Energy Transfer: When a car is detected, electrical energy stored in the road is transmitted to the tires using inductive coupling (like wireless charging pads).

Frictional Motion & Energy: The energy transfer can be modeled using P = F * v, where F is the force of friction and v is the vehicle speed. Assuming a tire-road contact force of 150 N and a speed of 20 m/s:

P = 150 N √ó 20 m/s = 3000 Watts (or 3 kW per vehicle at peak efficiency)

Total Energy = 100 m √ó 3 m √ó 8 kWh = 2400 kWh/day

That could power hundreds of electric vehicles daily depending on usage patterns.

Material Choices: For effective energy collection and durability, solar road panels would be built with a base aluminum sheet for support, layered with solar cells, and protected by tempered glass or transparent concrete. The glass includes anti-slip and anti-scratch coatings to ensure vehicle safety while allowing light to reach the photovoltaic cells below.

Problems & Solutions

Winter Weather Challenge (Canada): Snow and ice make roads slippery and dangerous in winter. With solar smart roads, energy harvested during sunny months can be stored and used to heat the road surface in winter‚Äîmelting snow and ice automatically.

This eliminates the need for plows in many areas and reduces traffic and accidents.

Water Management: All the melted snow becomes runoff. Integrated drainage systems beneath the road will direct the water into underground sewers, which then send it to purification plants for reuse or safe environmental release.

This makes the entire system more sustainable, reducing pollution and reusing a valuable natural resource: water.

Durability Challenge: Roads face heavy wear from weather and traffic. The materials we use‚Äîlike laminated tempered glass‚Äîmust resist cracking, scratching, and yellowing. Transparent polymers or composites may be explored in the future, but for now, glass offers the best compromise between clarity and toughness.

Regular maintenance would include surface cleaning and modular panel replacement, much like today's LED streetlights or sidewalk pavers.
